Shaking Inversed Spears

Hamlet

Such was the very armour he had on
When he the ambitious Norway combated.
(Act 1, Scene 1)

such is fronted to refer to something that has already been mentioned (that exact armour). the word order is reversed (OSV). this model is often used by Shakespeare to create a transition into the following sentences

the second line is built around the SOV structure, which is mainly used to shift the end emphasis to the verb. the important point is that he had combated the ambitious Norway and not that it was Norway that he stood up against

I your commission will forthwith dispatch,
And he to England shall along with you.
(Act 3, Scene 3)

one the first line, the word order is reversed (SOV) and the adverb is switched with the verb, the emphasis being placed on "dispatch"

the second line (SO), appears to be lacking a verb. through inversion the end emphasis is placed on "you", which implies that they should stay together

Sonnet 18

Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimmed,
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance, or nature’s changing course untrimmed:
So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.

in the first three lines inversion helps Shakespeare to achieve rhyme by pairing "declines" with "shines" (otherwise "too hot")

through fronting "as long as" becomes "so long as". it should be noted that on the last line "as" is omitted, while the subject/object positions are reversed

Sonnet 130

I have seen roses damask'd, red and white,
But no such roses see I in her cheeks;
And in some perfumes is there more delight
Than in the breath that from my mistress reeks.

lines two and three contain double inversions. subject-verb inversion and the object is put up front in the first. verb-there inversion and the complement is put up front in the second

inversion in the second line puts "cheeks" at the end, making it rhyme with the verb "reeks". in the third line inversion once again helps the rhyme, pairing "delight" with "white in the preceding line

Sonnet 33

Full many a glorious morning have I seen
Flatter the mountain-tops with sovereign eye,
Kissing with golden face the meadows green,
Gilding pale streams with heavenly alchemy;

the rest of the first line is fronted after the subject and the verb, relationship between which is also reversed. this moves "seen" to the end and pairs it up with "green". subject-complement inversion in the third line to create the rhyme

Macbeth

This is the sergeant
Who like a good and hardy soldier fought
'Gainst my captivity. Hail, brave friend!
(Act 1, Scene 2)

the complement is reversed with the action. the sergeant is first of all good and hardy, that is why he fought. in the more standard word order sergeant may not actually be a good and hardy soldier, although he did fight like one

No more that thane of Cawdor shall deceive
Our bosom interest: go pronounce his present death,
And with his former title greet Macbeth.
(Act 1, Scene 2)

the adverb is fronted. this replaces the longer "shall never again" and shifts the emphasis from the identity of the thane to his betrayal. on the third line, an effect of suspense is created. until the last word we do not know who will be the one who receives the title

If’t be so, For Banquo’s issue have I fil’d my mind,
For them the gracious Duncan have I murther’d,
Put rancors in the vessel of my peace
Only for them, and mine eternal jewel
Given to the common enemy of man,
To make them kings -the seed of Banquo kings!
Rather than so, come, Fate, into the list,
And champion me to the utterance!
(Act 3, Scene 1)

the inversions above show how distorted Macbeth's thinking is and highlight the conflict within his mind that will eventually drive him to insanity. he is not thinking so much of the actions as trying to justify the reasons behind them
