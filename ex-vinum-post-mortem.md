I wrote ex vinum because I'd never played a table-top rpg, but I was autistic enough to already want to create one. Also, my girlfriend mentioned she wanted to play D&D with me.

And that's when all justifications fade into the sweet nothingness of love. I wanted her to have fun, the rest didn't matter.

A few weeks later, I had typeset a 10 page rulebook in latex.

### self-critique

> It had to serve as a good introduction to the genre. Easy to grasp, little focus on numbers. Also, it had to ease the transition to D&D if we kept playing.

I had moderate success with this. She got a hang of it pretty quickly, although ended up forgoing certain mechanics.

> It had to feature a campaign she could get into, I would have fun writing, and one that wouldn't bore my friends. 

I was afraid it'd be too short. That was a mistake that made the narrative too involved.  At the same time, it came to a halt in the middle because of a lack of directions. 

> Also, easy enough to DM, as I've never done that before.

I tried to compensate my lack of DM experience by preparing well, and ended up overdoing it. Things went south, lines of dialogues were left unread.

> There had to be at least one character with interesting things to say.

Guess what, he spent half the campaign eating dirt. He didn't get to be what I wanted him to be because I imagined him in specific circumstances.

> Dungeon

> Combat

> And most importantly, gf had to like it.

Later that night, she asked me if I was happy with how it went, and said she had a great time. Needless to say, my heart melted.

But let's get back to the cold rationale.

Lessons learned:

- Know when to stop, don't over-prepare, and keep it concise.
- It's better to improvise and adapt setbacks than make them hardcoded plot devices.
- Construct characters with values, goals, and fears, improvise their dialogue.

As a final verdict, I'll give it 7/10.
